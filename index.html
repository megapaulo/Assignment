Pounded Yam and Egusi soup Recipe

Brief Description About Pounded yam and egusi

Pounded yam is a West African dish made from boiled yam that is pounded into a smooth, dough-like consistency. It's a staple food in many West African countries, particularly in Nigeria, where it's known as "iyan".

Egusi is made of fat- and protein-rich seeds of certain cucurbitaceous plants (squash, melon, gourd), which after being dried and ground are used as a major ingredient in West African cuisine. In Nigeria, Egusi soup is one of the most popular soups used for a variety of occasions. Egusi soup is a staple in homes and bukas, or street food stalls, across Nigeria and in many parts of West Africa.

Recipe Details Section

  • Prep time: 20 minutes
  • Cook time: 30 minutes
  • Servings: 4 plates
  • Difficulty: Beginner

Ingredients Section

  • Pounded Yam
    • Tuber of yam.
    • water as needed.
  • Egusi Soup
    • 2 cups grounded egusi (melon)
    • 1 cup palm oil or less if you desire
    • 2 tablespoons of neutral oil
    • 1/4 cup crayfish
    • 3 tablespoon locust beans (dried or wet)
    • 24 oz of chopped spinach
    • 1 tomato optional, 2 Red Bell Pepper
    • 1 Fresh Pepper
    • 2 onions sliced
    • 1 lb smoked turkey you may use other meat like tripe, beef, or seafood
    • Salt to taste
    • Chicken stock

    Instructions Section

    1. Pounded Yam
      1. Pounding Using motar and pestle.
        • Peel your yam and wash thoroughly, cut into 1-2 inch cubes.
        • Bring to boil in a large pot with water.
        • Wash the pestle and mortar for pounding.
        • Pound until smooth nead consistency is achieved.
      2. Using machine yam pounder.
        • Peel your yam and wash thoroughly, cut into 1-2 inch cubes.
        • Bring to boil in a large pot with water, and cook until soft.
        • Place in machine until desired result is obtained.
        • Once the consistency is to your liking, divide into pieces, place each piece on top of plastic wrap and use the plastic wrap to encase the kneaded yam forming a ball.
        • Eat your pounded yam with your egusi
    2. Egusi Soup
      • Mix together the grounded egusi (melon) with equal parts water and set aside to hydrate.
      • In a pot add your smoked turkey and water and boil on low heat for up to 45 minutes to heat through, soften the turkey. Top up with water as needed.
      • Season with salt to taste.
      • Blend the bell pepper, and hot pepper and tomato if using together until smooth and set aside.
      • Heat your palm oil in a large pot or deep pan and allow it to melt over medium heat but don’t overheat the oil as your sliced onion and saute for about 3 minutes.
      • Add the blended pepper and cook reduced stirring often so it doesn’t burn.
      • Add your turkey stock from your boiling smoked turkey or other meat stock, and simmer on low stirring periodically so it does not burn.
      • In a large spoon, place egusi paste on top of your stew until you have used it all up. Reduce heat to low, cover pot and allow it to cook for about 20 to 30. Gently stir periodically again to prevent burning but in uniform strokes trying to avoid breaking up egusi too much until it is cooked.
      • Add your turkey or chicken meat.
      • Add your, locust beans, garlic and ginger paste and cray fish powder. Let it cook for about 5 minutes on medium heat, stirring so it doesn’t burn.
      • Stir in the spinach and leave to simmer for about 5 minutes. Adjust seasoning if necessary
      • Serve hot with your pounded yam.

      Additional Information

      Notes

      To make a creamier egusi, in Step 2, add an additional 1/2 cup water to the blender, along with the ground egusi seeds. In Step 3, stir all of the paste into the soup at once, then proceed as directed.

      You can find ground egusi seeds in many West African stores and online

      You will find pumpkin leaves, also known as ugwu or ugu, in Nigerian and West African stores. Curly kale and collard greens are acceptable substitutes.

      Storage

      The simmered beef and stock can be refrigerated in separate airtight containers for up to 4 days, or frozen for up to 3 months (thaw before using).

      Egusi soup can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 3 days or frozen for up to 1 month.
